GENERAL DATA PROTECTION REGULATION (GDPR) — EU 2016/679
Applicable to: Any AI system processing data of EU residents, or organizations
with EU operations. Adopted as a global best-practice standard for data governance.

=======================================================================
CHAPTER I — GENERAL PROVISIONS
=======================================================================

ARTICLE 1 — Subject-matter and objectives
GDPR protects natural persons with regard to the processing of their personal data
and ensures the free movement of personal data.

ARTICLE 4 — Definitions
- Personal data: Any information relating to an identified or identifiable natural person.
- Processing: Any operation performed on personal data (collection, storage, use, disclosure).
- Controller: The entity that determines the purposes and means of processing.
- Processor: The entity that processes data on behalf of the controller.
- Data subject: The natural person whose data is processed.
- Consent: Freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous indication of agreement.
- Special categories: Racial/ethnic origin, political opinions, religious beliefs,
  genetic data, biometric data, health data, sexual orientation.

=======================================================================
CHAPTER II — PRINCIPLES (ARTICLE 5)
=======================================================================

ARTICLE 5 — Principles relating to processing of personal data
All personal data must be:
  (a) Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency: Processed lawfully, fairly, and transparently.
  (b) Purpose limitation: Collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes only.
      Must not be further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes.
  (c) Data minimisation: Adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary.
      AI systems must not collect more data than needed for loan decisions.
  (d) Accuracy: Kept accurate and up to date. Inaccurate data must be erased or rectified.
  (e) Storage limitation: Kept no longer than necessary for the purposes.
      Loan records must have defined retention periods.
  (f) Integrity and confidentiality: Processed with appropriate security,
      protecting against unauthorised processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage.
  (g) Accountability: The controller is responsible for and must demonstrate compliance.

=======================================================================
CHAPTER II — LAWFULNESS OF PROCESSING (ARTICLE 6)
=======================================================================

ARTICLE 6 — Lawfulness of processing
Processing is lawful only if at least one of the following applies:
  (a) Consent: The data subject has given consent for one or more specific purposes.
  (b) Contract: Processing is necessary for a contract to which the data subject is party.
  (c) Legal obligation: Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation.
  (d) Vital interests: Processing is necessary to protect vital interests.
  (e) Public task: Processing is necessary for performance of a public interest task.
  (f) Legitimate interests: Processing is necessary for legitimate interests of the controller,
      unless overridden by the data subject's fundamental rights.

ARTICLE 9 — Processing of special categories of personal data
Processing of biometric data, health data, financial vulnerability data is prohibited
unless explicit consent is obtained, or it falls under a specific exemption.

=======================================================================
CHAPTER III — RIGHTS OF DATA SUBJECTS
=======================================================================

ARTICLE 12 — Transparent information and communication
The controller must provide information in a concise, transparent, intelligible,
and easily accessible form. Requests must be responded to within ONE MONTH.

ARTICLE 13 — Right to be informed (data collected directly)
When collecting data, inform the data subject of:
  - Controller identity and contact
  - Purposes and legal basis for processing
  - Recipients of the personal data
  - Data retention period
  - Existence of automated decision-making and meaningful information about logic involved
  - Rights available to the data subject

ARTICLE 14 — Right to be informed (data not collected directly)
Where data was not collected directly from the data subject, they must be informed
within a reasonable period (at most 1 month).

ARTICLE 15 — Right of access
Data subjects have the right to obtain confirmation of whether their personal data
is being processed, and if so, access to that data and a copy.
AI systems must provide a /rights/access endpoint.

ARTICLE 16 — Right to rectification
Data subjects may request correction of inaccurate personal data without undue delay.

ARTICLE 17 — Right to erasure ('Right to be forgotten')
Data subjects may request erasure of personal data when:
  (a) Data is no longer necessary for the purposes it was collected.
  (b) Consent is withdrawn and there is no other legal ground for processing.
  (c) Data subject objects to processing and there are no overriding legitimate grounds.
  (d) Personal data has been unlawfully processed.
  (e) Erasure is required by Union or Member State law.
AI systems must provide a /rights/erasure endpoint and process requests promptly.

ARTICLE 18 — Right to restriction of processing
Data subjects may restrict processing in certain circumstances (e.g., disputing accuracy).

ARTICLE 20 — Right to data portability
Data subjects may receive their personal data in a structured, machine-readable format
and transmit it to another controller.

ARTICLE 21 — Right to object
Data subjects may object to processing at any time, including profiling for
direct marketing purposes. The AI must cease processing upon a valid objection.

ARTICLE 22 — Automated individual decision-making including profiling
Data subjects have the right NOT to be subject to a decision based solely on
automated processing that significantly affects them, including profiling.
EXCEPTIONS: Allowed only if:
  (a) Necessary for a contract with the data subject.
  (b) Authorised by Union or Member State law.
  (c) Based on explicit consent.
REQUIREMENT: In cases (a) and (c), the controller must implement suitable safeguards,
including the right to obtain human intervention, express their point of view,
and contest the decision.
Applicable when: Every AI loan decision (approved/rejected) made automatically
must disclose the logic used and offer a human review option.

=======================================================================
CHAPTER IV — CONTROLLER AND PROCESSOR OBLIGATIONS
=======================================================================

ARTICLE 24 — Responsibility of the controller
Controllers must implement appropriate technical and organisational measures
to ensure processing is performed in accordance with GDPR.

ARTICLE 25 — Data protection by design and by default
Controllers must implement data protection principles at the time of designing
the system and by default only process data necessary for each specific purpose.

ARTICLE 30 — Records of processing activities
Controllers must maintain records of processing activities including:
  - Purposes of processing
  - Categories of data subjects and personal data
  - Recipients
  - Retention periods
  - Technical and organisational security measures

ARTICLE 32 — Security of processing
Implement appropriate measures including:
  - Pseudonymisation and encryption of personal data
  - Ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability of systems
  - Regular testing, assessing, and evaluating security measures

ARTICLE 33 — Notification of breach to supervisory authority
Controllers must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of
becoming aware of a personal data breach.

ARTICLE 35 — Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)
Required before processing that is likely to result in a high risk to data subjects'
rights. Mandatory for: systematic profiling, large scale processing of special
categories, systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas.

=======================================================================
CHAPTER VIII — REMEDIES, LIABILITY AND PENALTIES
=======================================================================

ARTICLE 77 — Right to lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority
Data subjects may lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority.

ARTICLE 79 — Right to an effective judicial remedy
Data subjects have the right to an effective judicial remedy against a controller.

ARTICLE 82 — Right to compensation and liability
Any person who has suffered material or non-material damage as a result of
an infringement has the right to receive compensation from the controller or processor.

ARTICLE 83 — General conditions for imposing administrative fines
Tier 1 violations (Articles 8, 11, 25–39, 42, 43):
  Up to €10 million, or 2% of total worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher).
Tier 2 violations (Articles 5, 6, 7, 9, 12–22, 44–49):
  Up to €20 million, or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover (whichever is higher).
